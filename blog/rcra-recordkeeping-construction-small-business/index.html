RCRA Recordkeeping Requirements for Small Construction Companies

The Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (RCRA) regulates hazardous waste from cradle to grave—including construction sites. Learn who it applies to, what records you must keep, and how to avoid penalties up to $81,540 per day.

What Is RCRA?

The Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (RCRA) is a federal law enacted in 1976 that governs the management of hazardous and non-hazardous solid waste in the United States. It's administered by the EPA and enforced at both federal and state levels.

RCRA's core principle: Hazardous waste must be tracked from the moment it's generated until it's properly disposed of ("cradle to grave"). This includes construction waste.

Why Construction Companies Fall Under RCRA

Most construction companies assume RCRA only applies to chemical plants or manufacturing facilities. Wrong. Construction activities generate multiple types of hazardous waste:

  • Lead paint debris (from pre-1978 buildings)
  • Asbestos-containing materials (insulation, floor tiles, roofing)
  • Contaminated soil (petroleum, heavy metals)
  • Mercury-containing bulbs and fixtures
  • Solvents and adhesives (paint thinners, epoxy)
  • Treated wood waste (CCA-treated lumber)
Common misconception: "We're just a small construction company—we don't deal with hazardous waste."

If you've demolished a pre-1978 building, removed old insulation, or disposed of contaminated soil, you've handled RCRA-regulated waste—whether you knew it or not.

Who Does RCRA Apply To? Generator Categories Explained

RCRA divides waste generators into three categories based on the quantity of hazardous waste generated per month. Your category determines your regulatory obligations.

Generator Category Determination

Generator Type Hazardous Waste/Month Typical Construction Example Recordkeeping Required?
VSQG
(Very Small Quantity Generator)		 < 100 kg
(~220 lbs or 25 gallons)		 Solo contractor occasionally removing lead paint debris from small remodel jobs ❌ Minimal (optional)
SQG
(Small Quantity Generator)		 100-1,000 kg/month
(220-2,200 lbs)		 Mid-size contractor doing 2-3 demolitions per month with lead/asbestos ✅ Yes (3 years)
LQG
(Large Quantity Generator)		 > 1,000 kg/month
(> 2,200 lbs)		 Commercial contractor with multiple job sites generating contaminated soil, asbestos, lead paint ✅ Yes (3-5 years, extensive)

How to Determine Your Category

Step 1: Calculate monthly hazardous waste generation

  • Track waste from all job sites for 1 month
  • Only count hazardous waste (not regular construction debris)
  • Include: lead paint debris, asbestos, contaminated soil, solvents, treated wood

Step 2: Apply the thresholds

  • < 220 lbs = VSQG (minimal regulations)
  • 220-2,200 lbs = SQG (moderate regulations, 3-year records)
  • > 2,200 lbs = LQG (full regulations, 5-year records, EPA ID number required)
Example calculation:

You demolish a 1950s house with lead paint and asbestos. You generate:

  • 500 lbs lead paint debris
  • 300 lbs asbestos floor tiles
  • 150 lbs contaminated soil
  • Total: 950 lbs hazardous waste

Result: You're an SQG for that month. You must keep RCRA records for 3 years.

Specific Recordkeeping Requirements for Construction

For VSQGs (Very Small Quantity Generators)

Good news: VSQGs have minimal federal recordkeeping requirements. However:

  • State regulations may be stricter—check your state's environmental agency
  • Best practice: Keep basic disposal records anyway (hauler name, date, waste type) to protect yourself in case of complaints or audits

For SQGs (Small Quantity Generators) - Most Common for Construction

If you're an SQG, you must maintain these records for at least 3 years:

1. Hazardous Waste Manifests

A manifest is a multi-part form (EPA Form 8700-22) that tracks hazardous waste from your site to the disposal facility. You must keep:

  • A copy of every manifest you sign
  • Proof of final disposal (signed copy from the disposal facility)
  • Retention: 3 years from date of shipment

2. Biennial Reports (If Required by State)

Some states require SQGs to file biennial reports detailing:

  • Types and quantities of hazardous waste generated
  • Where waste was sent for disposal
  • Retention: 3 years

3. Employee Training Records

If you have employees handling hazardous waste, you must document:

  • Date of training
  • Topics covered (e.g., "Hazardous waste identification and handling")
  • Trainer name and qualifications
  • Retention: 3 years from date of training

4. Waste Determination Documentation

You must document how you determined waste is hazardous:

  • Lab test results (e.g., lead paint chips analyzed for lead content)
  • Generator knowledge records (e.g., "Building constructed 1952, pre-dates lead paint ban, therefore presumed to contain lead-based paint")
  • Retention: 3 years

5. Exception Reports

If you don't receive a signed manifest back from the disposal facility within 60 days, you must file an exception report with the EPA. Keep copies of:

  • The original manifest
  • Your exception report submission
  • Retention: 3 years

For LQGs (Large Quantity Generators)

LQGs face extensive recordkeeping requirements beyond SQGs:

  • All SQG requirements (above)
  • Contingency plan: Written emergency procedures for spills/accidents
  • Weekly inspections: Documented inspections of waste storage areas
  • Annual reports: Detailed waste generation and disposal data
  • Personnel training: Annual refresher training documented
  • Retention: 5 years (not 3)
LQGs must obtain an EPA ID number before generating hazardous waste. Operating without an EPA ID is a serious violation—potential criminal penalties apply.

Common RCRA Violations and Penalties

Penalty Structure (As of 2024)

The EPA adjusts penalties annually for inflation. Current maximum civil penalties:

  • Base fine: $81,540 per day per violation
  • Multiple violations: Fines stack (e.g., 3 violations = $244,620/day)
  • Criminal penalties: Up to $50,000/day + 5 years in prison for knowing violations

Real-World Penalty Examples (2023-2024)

Case 1: Texas Demolition Contractor (2023)
  • Violation: Failed to keep manifest records for asbestos disposal
  • Penalty: $125,000 fine + required environmental audit ($40,000)
  • Lesson: Lack of records = presumption of improper disposal
Case 2: Florida Commercial Builder (2024)
  • Violation: Stored lead paint debris for 270 days (SQG limit: 180 days)
  • Penalty: $45,000 fine + mandatory corrective action
  • Lesson: Storage time limits are strictly enforced
Case 3: California Remodeling Company (2023)
  • Violation: Disposing of lead-contaminated soil in regular landfill (not RCRA-permitted)
  • Penalty: $380,000 fine + criminal misdemeanor charges for owner
  • Lesson: Improper disposal = criminal liability, not just civil fines

Most Common Construction RCRA Violations

  1. No manifest records (46% of violations)
  2. Exceeding storage time limits (22% of violations)
  3. Improper waste labeling/marking (18% of violations)
  4. No EPA ID number (LQGs) (8% of violations)
  5. No employee training documentation (6% of violations)

Small Business Exemptions and Conditional Exclusions

VSQG Exemptions

If you consistently generate < 220 lbs/month of hazardous waste, you're exempt from:

  • EPA ID number requirement
  • Manifest system (for most waste types)
  • Biennial reporting

However, you still must:

  • Identify hazardous waste correctly
  • Use a licensed waste hauler or disposal facility
  • Comply with state-specific regulations (which may be stricter)

Universal Waste Rule

Certain common wastes have simplified regulations under the Universal Waste Rule:

  • Batteries (lead-acid, lithium, nickel-cadmium)
  • Mercury-containing equipment (thermostats, fluorescent bulbs)
  • Pesticides (recalled or unused)
  • Lamps (fluorescent, HID, neon)

Benefits: Longer storage time limits (1 year), no manifests required for small quantities, easier disposal options.

Catch: You still need records of disposal (who, when, where, how much).

Household Waste Exclusion

Waste from residential customers (homeowners) is often exempt from RCRA even if it's technically hazardous. Example:

  • You're hired by a homeowner to remove lead paint from their personal residence
  • The waste is exempt from RCRA generator requirements (but may still be subject to state regulations)
Important distinction: This exemption only applies to waste from residential households, not commercial buildings, rental properties, or multi-family units. Check with your state environmental agency.

Required Records Checklist

Use this checklist to ensure you're keeping all necessary RCRA records:

  • Hazardous waste manifests (EPA Form 8700-22 or state equivalent)
  • Signed manifests from disposal facility (proof of receipt)
  • Waste determination documents (lab results or generator knowledge records)
  • Employee training records (date, topics, trainer)
  • Biennial reports (if SQG or LQG in reporting state)
  • Exception reports (if manifest not returned within 60 days)
  • EPA ID number documentation (LQGs only)
  • Contingency plan (LQGs only)
  • Inspection logs (LQGs only)
  • Hauler licenses/permits (verify hauler is authorized)

Compliance Quick-Start Guide

Step 1: Determine Your Generator Category

  1. Review past 12 months of projects
  2. Estimate monthly hazardous waste generated
  3. Classify yourself: VSQG, SQG, or LQG

Step 2: Set Up Recordkeeping System

Manual system (free):

  • Create project binders with waste disposal section
  • File manifests, invoices, lab results
  • Store for 3-5 years (depending on generator category)

Digital system ($149/year):

  • Use ComplianceLogger or similar app
  • Scan manifests, upload photos of waste
  • Automatic retention period tracking
  • Audit-ready reports in minutes

Step 3: Train Your Team

  • Conduct annual training on hazardous waste identification
  • Document training (date, attendees, topics)
  • Keep records for 3 years

Step 4: Vet Your Haulers

Before hiring a waste hauler, verify:

  • They have proper EPA/state permits
  • They use RCRA-permitted disposal facilities
  • They provide complete manifests
Pro tip: Ask haulers for references from other construction companies. A reputable hauler will have no problem providing them.

The Bottom Line

RCRA recordkeeping isn't optional—it's federal law. The good news: most construction companies are VSQGs or SQGs, which have manageable recordkeeping requirements.

Key takeaways:

  • Determine your generator category (VSQG/SQG/LQG)
  • Keep manifest records for 3-5 years
  • Use licensed haulers and RCRA-permitted disposal facilities
  • Document employee training annually
  • Start tracking before EPA shows up for an audit

The cost of compliance ($0-500/year for basic recordkeeping) is trivial compared to penalties ($45,000-380,000+ for violations). Don't wait for an audit to get your records in order.
